EXPERIMENTAL — the investments module is still maturing; expect rough edges.

The one-minute version

This tool answers one question: how are my investments actually doing, given when each dollar went in and came out. It tracks every account you hold money in — retirement, savings/brokerage, and alternatives — and computes real, timing-adjusted returns (XIRR), not just "total gain."

It's built on two ideas: lots (each chunk of money you put in) and events (everything that ever happened to it). You mostly won't think about either — you just record what happened, and the math follows.

Lots & events — the two building blocks

A lot is one of "your $10Ks." Every dollar of net-new money you invest becomes a lot with a sticky origin date and origin amount. Its value grows or shrinks over time. The important trick: when money moves between investment accounts, the lot moves with its origin — so $7K you put in a CD in 2016 is still "the 2016 $7K" after it rolls into your brokerage, and its return clock keeps running from 2016.

An event is a dated record of something that happened: a contribution, a withdrawal, a move between accounts, a value snapshot, a dividend/interest/fee, or an account closing. Events are append-only — the ledger is the full history, and the numbers on every other page are derived from it.

Money in, money out, and moves — the three kinds of transfer

This is the part that ties into the rest of the app. Every dollar movement is exactly one of three things:

What happenedEvent typeCounts as new money?

Because a move is neither new money nor an expense, it never distorts your returns — portfolio XIRR only counts the dollars that truly entered or left the investment universe.

How it connects to the rest of the app

Your 2026-and-onward money movements already flow in from your Chase bank imports. When you categorize a checking transfer as going into an investment account, the Sync button on the Transactions page pulls it into this tracker as a contribution (or a withdrawal if it came back). That's why, going forward, you barely have to touch this tool — new activity arrives from the bank feed.

The one thing Sync deliberately does not pull is a move between two investment accounts (e.g. an IRA rollover), because that's not a checking transaction — you record those as a move here.

Snapshots are what keep it honest. Every time you enter a statement value, the tool spreads the difference vs its own number across your open lots. More snapshots = a better value chart, better per-lot accuracy, and a real time-weighted return. Year-end at a minimum; monthly if you can.

Worked example — HYSA → CD → back to HYSA

Say you move $10,000 out of your HYSA into a 12-month CD, it earns $400, and it matures back into the HYSA. Here's exactly what you'd record — each move is a single row that handles both the outflow and the inflow:

dateaccounttypeamountto_accountvaluewhat it does
2024-01-15HYSA 0001move10000CDTakes $10K out of HYSA, puts it in the CD (both sides, one row)
2025-01-15CDinterest400CD earned $400 — now worth $10,400
2025-01-16CDmove10400HYSA 0001Rolls the full $10,400 back into the HYSA
2025-01-17CDcloseCloses the CD account (only if you won't reuse it)

Key points: you do not write separate "out of HYSA" and "into CD" rows — the one move row does both. And you must record the $400 of interest before moving $10,400 back, otherwise the tool only knows about $10,000 in the CD and the move will be rejected (you can't move out more than the account holds). If you'd rather, replace the interest row with a snapshot of 10400 on maturity day — same effect, and snapshots are self-correcting.

Employer 401(k) contributions

Your employer 401(k) contributions come in through payroll, not as a checking transaction you initiate — so you should not have to enter them twice. Wherever they already land, the tool's job is to pick them up automatically; you'll never key an employer contribution by hand. (This auto-arrival + a clear "your money vs. employer money" split is the next thing being wired up — see the note on the Overview once it's live.)

The two return numbers — XIRR & TWR

XIRR — "what did my money actually earn?" Think of it as the interest rate on a savings account, but one that correctly handles the fact that you deposited money at different times. It's the single annual rate that, applied to each dollar for exactly as long as that dollar was invested, turns all your deposits into what you have today. It cares about both how much and when: a dollar you put in years ago had more time to compound, and a dollar you happened to add right before a run-up gets credit for that good timing. It's your real, timing-aware annual return.

Example: $10,000 in 2020 + $10,000 in 2022, now worth $30,000. XIRR asks "what yearly rate grows the first $10K (invested ~6 yrs) plus the second $10K (~4 yrs) into $30K?" → about 8% a year. Note it's not the naive "$10K gain on $20K = 50%," because the 2022 money wasn't invested the whole time.

TWR — "how did the investments themselves do, ignoring my deposit timing?" This is the number you'd use to judge a fund manager — they don't control when you add money, only how the investments perform. The trick to stripping out your timing: TWR chops the timeline into segments at each deposit/withdrawal, measures the percentage return of each segment on its own, then multiplies those percentages together. A deposit lands at a segment boundary — it adds principal but doesn't change the percentage the investments moved within a segment — so the size and timing of your deposits fall out of the math. (This is why TWR needs your snapshots: it has to know the account's value at each segment boundary.)

Same example, and note TWR is not the flat 50%. Say the first $10K had grown to $12K by the 2022 deposit: segment 1 (2020→2022) = +20%; you add $10K → $22K; segment 2 (2022→now) $22K→$30K = +36%. TWR = 1.20 × 1.36 − 1 = +63% total — the actual investment performance in each stretch, chained, unaffected by how big your 2022 deposit was.

Modified Dietz — the accurate flavor of TWR used here. A plain TWR needs a snapshot at every single cash flow. Since real life has flows between your snapshots, the tool uses Modified Dietz for each period: instead of dividing the period's gain by the starting value, it divides by the average capital that was actually at work — each flow is weighted by the fraction of the period it was invested. That stops a big mid-period transfer (like a rollover) from wildly distorting the number, and is the industry-standard way to approximate TWR between snapshots.

Why they differ, and what it tells you. When XIRR is higher than TWR, your timing helped — you tended to add money right before gains. When XIRR is lower, your timing hurt. Same investments, two lenses: TWR grades the investments; XIRR grades your actual experienced return including when you deposited. The more snapshots you add, the sharper both get.
